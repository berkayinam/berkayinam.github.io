A Cradle-to-Customer Life Cycle Assessment Case Study of UK Vertical Farming

Michael Gargaro, Astley Hastings, Richard J. Murphy, Zoe M. Harris
University of Surrey, Centre for Environment and Sustainability; University of Aberdeen
Journal of Cleaner Production, 470, 143324 (2024) — DOI: 10.1016/j.jclepro.2024.143324

Abstract

Global population rise, increased rural-to-urban migration, climate change and conflict have placed strain on global trade and food supply chains. Vertical farming (VF) is a relatively novel method for cultivating fresh fruits and vegetables, and is often claimed to have lower environmental impacts than field cultivation. However, to date there are few studies utilising primary data which have evaluated the environmental impact VF may have. This study utilised Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to evaluate the environmental impact of lettuce production in a commercial vertical farm in the UK, compared with field cultivation data from the literature. Scenarios were also examined to evaluate the impact of VF systems with varying electricity sources, and waste, water and nutrient management options.

1. Introduction

Vertical farming represents one of the most discussed innovations in sustainable agriculture. Proponents argue that VF reduces land use, eliminates pesticides, and enables local food production year-round. Critics highlight the high energy intensity and complex supply chains. This study fills a critical gap by conducting a comprehensive, primary-data LCA of a real UK commercial VF operation, covering the full cradle-to-customer boundary: construction materials, energy use during operation, water and nutrient inputs, waste handling, packaging, and customer delivery.

2. Methodology

The functional unit was 1 kg of lettuce (fresh weight) delivered to the consumer in the UK. The system boundary covered: raw material extraction for construction; transportation of inputs; operation (electricity, water, nutrients, CO₂ injection); waste management; packaging; and last-mile delivery. SimaPro v9.3 with EcoInvent 3.8 database was used for modelling. Impact categories assessed: climate change (GWP100), freshwater eutrophication, marine eutrophication, terrestrial acidification, water consumption, land use, and fossil resource scarcity.

3. Key Results

3.1 Climate Change

Under the current UK grid (2022 average: ~233 gCO₂eq/kWh), VF lettuce produced 3.9 kg CO₂eq/kg — similar to or lower than air-freighted field lettuce from southern Europe or North Africa (4–8 kg CO₂eq/kg) but 3–5× higher than UK field-grown lettuce (0.8–1.2 kg CO₂eq/kg). Switching to 100% renewable electricity reduced VF GWP to 0.6 kg CO₂eq/kg, outperforming all field scenarios.

3.2 Other Environmental Categories

VF scored significantly better on land use (98% reduction vs. field), water use (95% reduction), and pesticide-related toxicity. However, freshwater eutrophication and acidification were slightly higher due to nutrient solution production and LED manufacturing supply chains.

3.3 Hotspot Analysis

Electricity consumed during operation accounted for 78% of total GWP. The substrate ('plug') used for seedling establishment contributed 8% of GWP and 35% of eutrophication impact — an unexpected hotspot. Packaging contributed 6% of GWP.

4. Scenario Analysis

  • Renewable electricity: GWP reduced from 3.9 to 0.6 kg CO₂eq/kg (−85%).
  • Nutrient recirculation: Eutrophication reduced by 22%.
  • Biodegradable plugs: Eutrophication reduced by 31%.
  • Local delivery optimisation: Last-mile transport GWP reduced by 40%.

5. Conclusions

VF can achieve competitive or superior climate performance compared with imported field produce when powered by renewable electricity. Energy source is the single most important determinant of VF environmental performance. Investment in renewable energy procurement and on-site generation should be the industry's top priority. Secondary priorities include substrate substitution and nutrient management. These findings provide the empirical evidence needed to underpin VF sustainability claims and guide policy on low-carbon food systems.

Reference: Gargaro M. et al. (2024). A cradle-to-customer life cycle assessment case study of UK vertical farming. Journal of Cleaner Production, 470, 143324.
