Introduction

As Saffron ( Crocus sativus L .) is

primarily cultivated in Iran, which accounts for

over 90% of the world's production. Other

significant saffron -producing countries include

Kashmir in India, Spain, Morocco, Greece, and

Italy. The global saffron production is estimated

to be around 418 tons per year, cultivated on an

area of approximately 121,338 hectares. In the

Jammu and Kashmir region, the total area under

saffron cultivation is 3,715 hectares, with a production of 16 metric tons and a productivity

ranging from 3.0 to 4.0 kg per hectare (Source: CSIR -IIIM).

However, saffron production in Kashmir has dropped significantly, with a 68% decline

over the past two decades. Data from the Agricultural Department shows that the area under

saffron cultivation decreased from 5,707 hectares in 1996 -97 to 2,387.71 hectares in 2018 -19.

Similarly, the production of saffron has fallen from 15.95 metric tonnes in the 1990s to just 2.6

metric tonnes in 2023 -24. Saffron was once widely cultivated in the Pulwama district, but

various factors, including low yields and increased urbanisation, have prompted farmers to

switch to higher -yielding crops such as apples and walnuts. Today, saffron cultivation is

confined mainly to Pampore and its surrounding areas. To address this problem indoor

cultivation of saffron is adopted. Indoor farming reduces water and chemical usage, offers

better control over environmental conditions, and requires less labor.

for its application in food, cosmetic sector and pharmaceuticals. The high prices of the saffron,

coupled with the time -consuming approach used in the cultivation, classifica tion as well as

harvesting makes the saffron market a highly profitable yet competitive one. A normal farming

practice is constrained with conditions such as weather and seasons that indoor cultivation does

away with.

In Saffron’s demand spans multiple industries, making it an appealing target for a diverse

consumer base:

• Culinary Industry: Saffron is prized for its unique flavor and coloring properties in

cuisines worldwide, particularly in Mediterranean, Indian, and Middle Eastern dishes.

Competitive Landscape:

The saffron industry is dominated by traditional growers, particularly in Iran, which

accounts for over 90% of global production. Other key producers include Spain, India, and

Greece. These traditional businesses compete on price, often affected by fluctua ting yields due

to climate change and unpredictable weather patterns.

Emerging indoor cultivation businesses, however, are positioning themselves as competitors

by offering:

• Consistency in Supply: Indoor farming eliminates seasonal variability, allowing

yearround production.

• Quality Control: Controlled environments enable growers to optimize growing

conditions, potentially producing higher -grade saffron.

• Sustainability: As environmental concerns rise; indoor cultivation’s lower water uses

and reduced land requirements appeal to eco -conscious consumers.

• While traditional producers compete primarily on scale and price, indoor cultivators

focus on product differentiation through consistency, quality, and sustainability. These

factors are becoming increasingly important in premium markets.

Target Market:

Target consumers involve up -scale culinary business such as restaurants and hotels,

cosmetics and pharma manufacturers and consumers in Europe, North America and in the

Middle East.

Benefits of indoor cultivation:

Traditional outdoor saffron cultivation, mostly in Kashmir, faces challenges such as

climate dependence, lower yields, and soil degradation. Indoor cultivation eliminates these

risks, providing a competitive edge with higher and more consistent yields.

Cultivation Plan:

Indoor cultivation of saffron using aeroponics in a “PUFF Unit (Polyurethane Foam

Unit)” is a highly innovative approach. Aeroponics allows saffron to grow in a soilless

environment, where the roots are suspended in air and misted with nutrient -rich water. Let’s

explore this in detail:

Puff Unit for Aeroponic Saffron Cultivation:

Puff Unit is a specialized growing chamber size 8 x 20 sq. feet that supports aeroponic

farming by maintaining optimal air, humidity, and misting conditions. It creates an ideal

Key Elements of a Puff Unit for Saffron Aeroponics:

• Suspended Corms in growing racks: The saffron corms are suspended in the air,

allowing their roots to hang freely. This setup ensures they are exposed to oxygen while

their roots receive periodic misting with a nutrient solution. Perforated trays are used

for carrying corms on perforated racks.

• Misting System: Aeroponic systems uses misters or humidifiers to deliver fine mist for

applying water and nutrients or maintaining relative humidity directly to the roots,

ensuring the plant gets optimal hydration and nourishment without water waste.

• Climate Control: Temperature and humidity are carefully managed using HVAC

systems to maintain ideal conditions. Automated sensors continuously monitor the

environment, adjusting temperature, humidity, and air circulation to prevent stress on

the plants and ensure optimal growth.

The Puff Unit provides precise control over:

• Temperature HVAC systems to maintain a stable temperature range between 15°C

and 20°C. Heating systems for drying are also essential.

• Humidity controlled by Humidifiers and dehumidifiers to maintain optimal moisture

levels, especially crucial during the growing and drying stages

• Ventilation System Proper airflow helps prevent mold and other fungal diseases.

Indoor setups should include fans or air exchange systems to keep air circulating.

• Carbon dioxide maintained by CO2 emitters.

• Light controlled by Efficient LED grow lights replicate sunlight, supporting

photosynthesis and the growth of the plant in an indoor setting. These can be

programmed for day -night cycles to mimic natural light conditions. LUX (250 to 300).

• Sensors are used to detect any sudden change and helps in controling the climate under

puff unit

This helps in mimicking the natural saffron -growing conditions. Saffron requires a period

of cold dormancy followed by warmer conditions for flowering.

Nutrient Solutions:

If required, the water mist is enriched with essential nutrients (e.g., phosphorus,

potassium) needed for saffron growth. The misting frequency can be adjusted to ensure optimal

absorption without waterlogging the corms.

Planting Materials:

Saffron corms with high quality without adulteration are used.

Harvesting:

Flowering begins in autumn, usually six to eight weeks after planting. Saffron flowers

bloom early in the morning, and harvesting should be done immediately to ensure the highest

quality. Each flower contains three crimson -red stigmas, which are carefully handpicked.

Harvesting Tools: Hand tools, tweezers, or specialized saffron harvesting tools for gently

picking the stigmas.

Drying:

Once harvested, the stigmas are dried to preserve flavor, aroma, and color. The drying

process requires a warm, dry environment, maintained at around 30 –35°C. Indoor systems use

temperature -controlled drying chambers, ensuring rapid yet gentle drying to ma intain saffron's

delicate qualities. Drying Equipment: Drying racks or temperature -controlled drying chambers

to ensure consistent drying of saffron stigmas without compromising their quality.

Benefits of Aeroponic Saffron Cultivation in a Puff Unit:

• Maximized Space Efficiency: The aeroponic system allows for vertical stacking of

trays, using minimal space. This can be especially useful in urban farming or

commercial indoor setups.

Cultivation Process: Indoor saffron cultivation unit 8 × 20 sq. feet (Procedure )

Indoor cultivation entails growing and planting of plants undertakers through controlled

propagation, planting, growing, harvesting and drying in a controlled environment.

From 15 August onwards:

• Corm procurement: Purchasing healthy corms from certified agencies or govt.

institutes for better establishment.

• Corm disinfection: The corms are treated with fungicide to prevent rotting and ensure

their health and viability.

• Sorting of corms: The corms are sorted based on their size (>6g for saffron production

& < 6g for multiplication) to ensure uniformity and optimal growth.

• Sowing or Placing corms in plastic trays or in soil: The corms are placed in plastic

trays for saffron production, or in soil (sand: silt: other organic material in 2:1:1 ratio)

for multiplication of corms, providing a suitable environment for their growth and

development.

• Transferring trays onto racks: The trays with corms are then transferred onto racks,

allowing for better organization and management of the cultivation process.

From 15 Oct. onwards:

• Dark phase: The racks with the trays of corms are placed in a dark environment for a

period of 120 days. This extended dark phase is necessary for the corms to undergo the

required physiological changes and prepare for sprouting and flowering.
