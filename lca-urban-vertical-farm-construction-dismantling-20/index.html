Life Cycle Assessment of an Urban Vertical Farm Benchmark: Construction to Dismantling and Recycling

Simeng Xie, Pedro Martinez-Vazquez, Charalampos Baniotopoulos
School of Engineering, Department of Civil Engineering, University of Birmingham, UK
Building and Environment (Elsevier), 2025 — DOI: 10.1016/j.buildenv.2025.111990

Abstract

Urban Vertical Farming helps mitigate problems associated with limited crop growth due to unstable land and climate conditions. Under suitable control—soilless technology, environmental control systems, automated management—food crops can be grown continuously without seasonal variations. However, the energy required to create optimum cultivation conditions leads to high operating costs and environmental impacts. This study discusses those issues in relation to the construction, operation and management of an urban vertical farm through life cycle analyses, considering scenarios that combine building materials supply, building design life, and transportation distance, while exploring ways to reduce carbon emissions through wind energy harvesting.

1. Introduction

Previous LCA studies of vertical farming have typically focused narrowly on the operational phase. This study takes a whole-life-cycle perspective — from construction of the facility through daily operation to eventual dismantling and material recycling — for a reference urban VF producing lettuce in a Birmingham, UK context. The inclusion of construction materials and end-of-life phases is novel and reveals carbon hotspots that operational analyses miss.

2. System Description and Methodology

The reference facility is a six-storey urban VF with 2,400 m² of growing area producing Lactuca sativa (butterhead lettuce) continuously across 26 crop cycles per year. SimaPro software was used with EcoInvent 3.9 database. The functional unit is 1 kg of fresh lettuce at the farm gate. Scenarios modelled included wind turbine integration, varying transport distances, and different structural material choices (steel vs. concrete).

3. Results

3.1 Global Warming Potential (GWP)

Under the current UK grid, the investigated urban VF yields a GWP of 5.43 kg CO₂eq per kilogram of lettuce — 9–14 times higher than traditional open field farming. The operational phase dominated at 91% of lifecycle GWP, with artificial lighting (LED arrays) and HVAC together responsible for 87% of operational emissions.

3.2 Construction Phase

Construction contributed 7% of lifecycle GWP. Steel structural framing had a 23% higher embodied carbon than a comparable concrete alternative, but concrete's higher weight increases foundation requirements. A hybrid structure achieved the best balance.

3.3 End-of-Life Phase

Dismantling and recycling recovered 4.06% of lifecycle carbon emissions. Recycling waste lettuce biomass at the use and production phase saved a further 3.61% of lifecycle carbon.

3.4 Wind Energy Integration

Installing a 50 kW rooftop wind turbine reduced operational GWP by 18%. Combined with grid decarbonisation to 2035 projections (50 gCO₂/kWh), VF GWP drops to 0.9 kg CO₂eq/kg — approaching parity with domestic field production.

4. Sensitivity Analysis

Transport distance for inputs and produce had marginal impact (<3%) compared with electricity source. Design life significantly affects amortised construction impact: a 30-year design life halves the annualised construction GWP compared with a 15-year scenario.

5. Conclusions

The dominant environmental hotspot in urban VF is artificial lighting and HVAC electricity consumption. Whole-lifecycle thinking reveals that construction materials and end-of-life recycling, while less impactful, are nonetheless worth optimising. Wind energy harvesting integrated into the building envelope offers a promising on-site renewable solution. The results highlight that grid decarbonisation is the single most powerful lever for making urban VF environmentally competitive with conventional farming.

Reference: Xie S., Martinez-Vazquez P., Baniotopoulos C. (2025). Life cycle assessment of an urban vertical farm benchmark from construction to dismantling and recycling. Building and Environment.
