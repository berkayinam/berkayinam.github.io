Benchmarking Energy Efficiency in Vertical Farming: Status and Prospects

Lorenzo Miserocchi, Alessandro Franco
Department of Energy, Systems, Territory, and Constructions Engineering (DESTEC), University of Pisa, Italy
Energy for Sustainable Development (Elsevier), 2024 — DOI: 10.1016/j.esd.2024.101507

Abstract

Energy efficiency is a critical yet challenging key performance indicator for vertical farming. The absence of a representative benchmark has thus far hindered comparison with traditional agricultural techniques. This paper establishes the current status and future prospects of energy efficiency in vertical farming by exploring the interplay between plant physiology and facility design and operation. Energy use is primarily driven by the need to control the physical environment—lighting, HVAC, irrigation—to maximise crop yield and quality.

1. Introduction

Vertical farming (VF) operates as a Controlled Environment Agriculture (CEA) system where all growth parameters—light, temperature, humidity, CO₂, nutrients—are precisely managed. While this enables year-round, location-independent production with minimal water use and no pesticides, it comes at a steep energy cost. Current VF facilities consume 100–2000 kWh per kg of fresh produce, far exceeding conventional agriculture's 2–10 kWh/kg, making energy efficiency the central challenge for commercial viability.

This benchmark study synthesises data from 40+ published VF installations and modelling studies to establish realistic energy use intensity (EUI) values and identify the key drivers of inefficiency.

2. Energy Breakdown in Vertical Farming

2.1 Lighting (50–70% of total energy)

Artificial lighting dominates VF energy consumption. LED systems have replaced fluorescent and HPS lamps, improving photon efficacy from ~1 μmol/J to 3–4 μmol/J. The daily light integral (DLI) for leafy greens is typically 12–17 mol/m²/day; higher DLI crops like tomatoes require 20–30 mol/m²/day and consequently much more energy per kg.

2.2 HVAC and Climate Control (20–35%)

Transpiration from plants drives latent heat loads; dehumidification is the dominant HVAC task in sealed VF rooms. Heat recovery from lighting and mechanical systems can reduce HVAC energy by 15–25%. Optimal temperature setpoints (18–24°C for most crops) and CO₂ enrichment (800–1200 ppm) together improve photosynthetic efficiency by up to 30%.

2.3 Irrigation and Nutrient Delivery (<5%)

Hydroponic and aeroponic delivery systems consume relatively little electricity but require careful management to avoid root-zone stress. Recirculating systems reduce water use by 90–95% compared with field cultivation.

3. Benchmark Results

Data from 44 case studies spanning leafy greens, herbs, strawberries, and microgreens:

  • Leafy greens: Median EUI 75–150 kWh/kg fresh weight; best-in-class ~35 kWh/kg.
  • Herbs (basil, mint): 200–400 kWh/kg due to higher DLI and slower growth.
  • Strawberries: 400–800 kWh/kg; economic viability requires premium pricing or renewables.
  • Carbon footprint: At average European grid intensity (300–400 gCO₂/kWh), VF lettuce produces 2–6 kg CO₂/kg — comparable to air-freighted field produce but 10× higher than domestic field lettuce.

4. Standardised KPI Framework

  • Energy Use Intensity (EUI): kWh per kg fresh weight or per m² per year.
  • PPFD efficiency: yield per mol of PAR light delivered.
  • COP: Coefficient of Performance for the HVAC subsystem.
  • Land Equivalent Ratio (LER): comparing VF with field area required for equivalent production.

5. Improvement Pathways

  1. Next-generation LEDs: Efficacy targets of 4.5–5 μmol/J achievable within 5 years; ~25% lighting energy reduction.
  2. Integrated heat recovery: Waste heat from LEDs recycled into HVAC reduces net energy by 10–20%.
  3. AI-driven adaptive lighting: Continuous spectrum and intensity adjustment cuts energy 15–30% without yield loss.
  4. Renewable energy integration: Pairing VF with solar PV and battery storage achieves near-zero-carbon operation.

6. Conclusions

Current best-practice EUI for leafy greens is ~35–75 kWh/kg, with a realistic 10-year target of 20–40 kWh/kg achievable through LED advances, heat recovery, and intelligent control. Standardising KPIs across the industry is essential to communicate environmental credentials and attract investment for scaling VF toward mainstream food production.
