Abstract: Hydroponics, a soilless cultivation method, relies on precise nutrient management

to optimize plant growth. This study provides a systematic scoping review of automated

hydroponic nutrient dosing systems, focusing on potential of hydrogen (pH) and electrical

conductivity (EC) dosing frameworks. Following preferred reporting items for system-

atic reviews and meta-analyses extension for systematic scoping reviews (PRISMA-ScR)

guidelines, 3222 studies were retrieved and screened, with 89 meeting inclusion criteria for

analysis. The review aimed to identify current research trends, dosing frameworks, critical

variables, and research gaps. Results reveal a steady rise in publications from 2015 ( n= 4) to

2022 ( n= 18). Feedback loop frameworks and predictive analytics are equally represented

(n= 45 each). Critical variables include pH ( n= 70), EC ( n= 36), total dissolved solids (TDS)

(n= 27), nutrient solution volume (NSV) ( n= 42), and nutrient solution temperature (NST)

(n= 28). The study highlights the need for robust frameworks incorporating advanced

dosing frameworks and simultaneous dosing strategies to enhance dosing speed, accuracy,

and robustness. A novel framework is proposed to address these gaps by integrating

predictive analytics using multiple regression models. This framework aims to improve the

dosing speed, accuracy, and robustness of automated hydroponic nutrient dosing systems.

The findings underscore the importance of further research into adaptive frameworks to

meet the growing demand for precision hydroponic systems.

1. Introduction

Hydroponics is a soilless cultivation method that supplies nutrients to the crops

through a closed-loop irrigation system in which the plant roots are usually submerged

in the nutrient solution [ 1,2]. The nutrient solution contains both macronutrients and

micronutrients, which are essential for the growth of plants [ 3]. Macronutrients and

micronutrients are consumed by plants in larger and smaller quantities, respectively, to

support various metabolic and cellular processes [ 4]. Therefore, the precision and efficiency

of the hydroponic nutrient solution dosing system are critical to ensure optimal crop

growth [ 5]. Research regarding hydroponic nutrient dosing systems usually consists of

the adjustment process of critical variables such as the potential of hydrogen (pH) and

electrical conductivity (EC). Furthermore, these variables are controlled in different values

depending on the optimal conditions required by specific crops [6,7].

The EC is a crucial parameter in hydroponics, as it directly influences nutrient avail-

ability, water uptake, and overall plant growth, making precise management essential for

optimal yield and quality. For instance, Wongsorn et al. examined the impact of different

EC levels (1, 2, 3, and 4 mS/cm) on the vegetative growth of Pepino (Solanum muricatum) .

Their results show that EC levels significantly influenced plant growth parameters. EC

levels of 2 and 3 mS/cm led to the highest chlorophyll fluorescence and leaf green index,

while an EC of 4 mS/cm resulted in the greatest plant height, canopy width, and stem

diameter. Lower EC levels (1 and 2 mS/cm) were associated with increased flowering

percentages [ 8]. In addition, research on lettuce grown in vertical hydroponic systems has

revealed that an EC level of 2.5 mS/cm produced the maximum yield, while an EC of

1.5 mS/cm resulted in the highest vitamin C and chlorophyll content [ 9]. This indicates that

different EC levels can optimize either yield or quality depending on the desired outcome.

On the other hand, the pH is also a critical factor in hydroponics. It directly affects

nutrient solubility and availability, root health, and overall plant growth. Thus, making

precise pH management essential for optimal crop performance. For example, Hopkin-

son et al. found that extreme pH levels (both very acidic and very basic) hindered the

growth of bush beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) . Plants grown in nutrient solutions with a pH of

5–6 exhibited healthier growth, while those at pH 8–9 showed stunted growth, thus reduc-

ing internode length and signs of nutrient deficiencies. The optimal pH range for bush

beans was identified as 5.6–6.2 to ensure adequate nutrient solubility and uptake [ 10].

Moreover, research on spinach grown hydroponically highlighted that maintaining a pH

range of 5.5–6.5 led to faster growth and better nutrient absorption. When pH deviated

from this range, plants exhibited pale yellow leaves and reduced growth due to insufficient

nutrient uptake [ 11]. Therefore, the growth of plants in hydroponics depends equally on

both pH and EC, as both parameters must be carefully regulated within their optimal

ranges as shown in Figure 1. Unfortunately, farmers often face challenges in maintaining

constant control over these variables [ 12]. Thus, the implementation of an automated

dosing system is becoming increasingly essential.

The significance of this study resides in its potential to address a gap in understanding

the framework utilized in hydroponic nutrient management to regulate the pH and EC.

The information from this study could inform future research as a guideline on how to

conduct or develop a hydroponic nutrient dosing system. Thus, this systematic scoping

1. Map the current research trends regarding hydroponic techniques concerning its

nutrient dosing operations,

2. Classify the hydroponic nutrient dosing framework employed in the hydroponic

nutrient dosing system,

3. Identify critical variables in the hydroponic nutrient dosing system,

4. Identify knowledge gaps regarding hydroponic nutrient dosing framework to inform

future research.

2. Materials and Methods

Systematic scoping reviews aim to pinpoint knowledge gaps methodically, offer

comprehensive overviews of existing literature, elucidate theoretical frameworks, and

evaluate the methodologies, approaches, and practices utilized in prior research studies [ 15].

Accordingly, this section delineates the methodologies employed in this study. Our method

adheres to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

(PRISMA) extension for systematic scoping reviews (PRISMA-ScR) guidelines [ 16], which

is a refined version of the original PRISMA framework established by Arksey et al. [ 17]. The

protocol serves as a directive, ensuring a structured and transparent process in reviewing

and assimilating the literature.

2.1. Research Questions

The articulation of the research question is essential as the guidelines for the entire

literature review process [ 18]. Following the recommendations by Levac et al. [ 19], the

research questions have been refined to align precisely with the objectives of the study and

facilitate a practical and relevant literature search. Therefore, the research questions are as

follows:

• What are the current research trends regarding hydroponic techniques and their

nutrient dosing operations?

• Which frameworks are predominantly used in conducting hydroponic nutrient

dosing operations?

• What variables are commonly considered in the hydroponic nutrient dosing framework?

• What methodological limitations and technological advancements exist in current

hydroponic dosing frameworks?

2.2. Information Source and Search Strategy

While the quality of research is not the primary concern in a systematic scoping review,

its scope must be comprehensive [ 18]. Thus, this study includes both peer-reviewed and

non-peer-reviewed documents. The literature search encompassed five diverse databases:

Web of Science (WoS), Scopus, ProQuest, IEEE Xplore, and Google Scholar. The selection of

keywords for the literature search is crucial and should derive from the research questions.

Additionally, the search terms can be expanded by incorporating synonyms, abbreviations,

alternative spellings, and related terms [ 18,20]. Therefore, the keyword strings in this study,

2.3. Eligibility Criteria and Data Extraction

The search was limited to articles in English. Only research articles were included,

while review articles were excluded. The relevance of the articles was assessed, focusing

on literature primarily related to hydroponic dosing frameworks. Searches were filtered

to publications from 2015 to September 2023. Data extraction should be conducive to

synthesizing answers to research questions. Some selected literature may contain only

some of the required information, but it was included in the study. An Excel workbook

was created to organize all extracted data, covering the following details: (1) Hydroponic

technique; (2) hydroponic dosing framework; (3) dosing variables. The data were charted,

synthesized, and summarized to highlight insights and identify research gaps for future

investigation.

3.2. Nutrient Dosing Framework

The nutrient dosing frameworks  in hydroponics  can be classi ﬁed into two domains:

feedback  loop (n = 45) and predictive  analytics  (n = 45), as illustrated  in Figure 4. The

feedback  loop framework  focuses on ongoing monitoring  and comparison  of current var-

iable values with predetermined  thresholds.  This domain encompasses  two methods:  the

conventional  feedback  loop (n = 42) and the proportional  integral derivative  (PID) (n = 3).

Wongsorn et al. [ 8] found that Pepino plants showed optimal growth at EC 2–3 mS/cm dur-

ing the vegetative stage, with EC requirements increasing to 3–4 mS/cm during flowering

and 4–5 mS/cm during fruiting. This correlated with a 65% increase in flower production

and 30% higher fruit biomass in controlled experiments. Complementing these findings,

Sangeetha & Periyathambi [ 104] developed machine learning frameworks that achieved

97.5% accuracy in identifying growth stages and 99.4% accuracy in nutrient delivery tim-

ing during the fruiting phase. Their work utilized Mask R-CNN algorithms for growth

stage detection through leaf morphology analysis, enabling precise, phase-specific nutrient

management. These advancements in phase-adjusted dosing strategies show promise for

significantly improving crop yields and reducing nutrient waste in hydroponic systems.

4. Research Gap

4.1. Temperature Compensation Model

The NST affects hydroponic nutrient solution pH and EC measurements [ 109,122].

The NST is essential for monitoring pH and EC in hydroponic systems. The standard

procedure is to adjust pH readings to 25◦C for consistency and accuracy [ 122]. Temperature

compensation formulas modify pH readings to temperature changes. The formula converts

pH measurements at any temperature to 25◦C, standardizing comparison and analysis.

We identified only one paper referencing a temperature correction factor for EC or pH

measurement. The inclusion of this component in other research, even NST studies, is

uncertain. Telaumbanua et al. [ 38] developed an EC measurement correction factor using

a linear regression model. Helmy et al. [ 57] found a clear relationship between TDS and

NST, emphasizing the necessity of temperature adaptation. These findings suggest adding

a temperature correction component to hydroponics research to improve measurement

accuracy. A thermoelectric Peltier temperature control system for the NST can also be

developed for this study [123].

5. Conclusions

Effective controls of hydroponic nutrient solutions are crucial for the success of hydro-

ponics cultivation. This systematic scoping review has outlined various frameworks for

hydroponic nutrient dosing operations by comprehensively reviewing 81 research articles

on the hydroponic nutrient dosing framework. These frameworks are categorized into

two domains: feedback loop and predictive analytics. Each domain influences whether

dosing operations are conducted sequentially or simultaneously, affecting the required

dosing time. The study emphasizes the importance of critical dosing variables, including

pH, EC or TDS, NST, and NSV . In detail, pH, EC, and TDS are vital variables that need to be

maintained within specific thresholds, while NSV is crucial when normalization procedures

are implemented. On the other hand, the measurement of NST is essential to include the

temperature compensation factor while measuring pH, EC, and TDS.

This comprehensive review also highlights the crucial significance and complex rela-

tionship between these dosing variables. However, these variables must be included in a

single dosing system to enhance performance. The insights gained from this review lay a

robust foundation for a thorough understanding of the various aspects of nutrient dosing

strategies, significantly enhancing agricultural practices in precision and efficiency.

However, this scoping review is limited to research articles, and including review

articles in future studies may provide additional insights into hydroponic nutrient dosing

systems. We discovered that the existing nutrient dosing frameworks need to be revised

in order to be robust. Future research is encouraged to utilize and optimize our proposed

dosing framework, which may offer significant improvements. Our proposed framework

is expected to significantly contribute to nutrient dosing operations’ robustness, accuracy,

and efficiency, representing a meaningful advancement in precision hydroponics. This

comprehensive approach aims to address current challenges and pave the way for more

efficient and effective hydroponic farming practices.
