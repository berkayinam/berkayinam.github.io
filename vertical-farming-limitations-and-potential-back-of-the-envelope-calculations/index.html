Vertical Farming Limitations and Potential: Back-of-the-Envelope Calculations

25 May 2026 4 min read
Soru & Cevap Destek ol

Authors: Samuel J Lovat, Εlad Noor, Ron Milo — Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, Weizmann Institute of Science, Rehovot 7610001, Israel

Journal: Plant Physiology, Volume 198, Issue 3, July 2025, kiaf056. doi: 10.1093/plphys/kiaf056

Published: 02 April 2025 | License: Open Access CC BY

Source: Oxford Academic — Plant Physiology

Abstract

Vertical farming — growing crops in stacked layers under artificial light in climate-controlled environments — promises to address food security challenges through higher yields, reduced land use, and year-round production. However, it faces major challenges including high energy consumption and capital costs. This article uses back-of-the-envelope calculations to assess where vertical farming can realistically deliver on its promises and where it faces fundamental limitations. The analysis covers energy requirements per kilogram of produce, land-use trade-offs, water efficiency, and the conditions under which vertical farming is economically and environmentally viable.

The Promise and Challenge of Vertical Farming

Vertical farming offers compelling advantages in theory: crops grown in stacked layers can produce yields 10–100 times higher per unit of land area compared to conventional agriculture. By growing in controlled environments, vertical farms can operate year-round regardless of climate, conserve up to 95% more water than field agriculture, and eliminate pesticide use. These characteristics make vertical farming particularly attractive for urban environments with limited land availability and for regions with harsh climates or water scarcity.

Energy: The Central Challenge

The fundamental constraint of vertical farming is its dependence on artificial lighting. Back-of-the-envelope calculations reveal that the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) required to grow crops demands substantial electricity. For leafy greens such as lettuce, energy consumption in current vertical farming facilities ranges from approximately 10–18 kWh per kilogram of produce, compared to near-zero direct energy input for field-grown crops. This energy intensity makes electricity cost and source the defining variable in vertical farming's environmental and economic viability.

Key calculations for energy efficiency: with current high-efficiency LED systems achieving approximately 3–4 μmol of PAR per joule of electricity, and assuming typical photoperiods of 16–18 hours per day, a leafy green crop producing around 4 kg/m² per cycle requires roughly 150–400 kWh/m² per year in lighting alone. Adding HVAC, pumping, and other systems typically increases total energy consumption by 40–60%.

When Does Vertical Farming Make Sense?

The calculations reveal that vertical farming is most advantageous for:

High-value, fast-growing crops: Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, herbs) with short growth cycles of 25–35 days maximize the revenue per unit of energy and capital invested. For crops with longer growth cycles or lower market values, the economics become challenging.

Electricity from renewable sources: When powered by renewable energy at low marginal costs (solar, wind), vertical farming's carbon footprint can approach or match conventional agriculture. With fossil-fuel-based electricity, vertical farming's CO₂ emissions per kilogram of produce can be 5–20 times higher than field cultivation.

Water-scarce regions: Vertical farming uses water approximately 95% more efficiently than field agriculture. In arid regions where water is expensive or scarce, this efficiency gain can justify the higher energy costs.

Urban proximity: Reducing transportation distances from farm to consumer can offset energy costs through lower distribution emissions and fresher produce with longer shelf life.

Fundamental Limitations for Staple Crops

The calculations demonstrate that vertical farming of calorie-dense staple crops (wheat, rice, potatoes) is not economically viable with current technology. The primary reason is the low economic value per unit of land and energy. For a staple crop yielding 5–10 kg of dry biomass per m² per year in field conditions, the energy required to replicate this production vertically would cost more than the market value of the crop itself.

Furthermore, the light-use efficiency of plants imposes a fundamental upper bound. Even with optimal LED spectra perfectly matched to plant photosynthesis, only about 8–12% of electricity can theoretically be converted to plant biomass energy. This thermodynamic constraint means that vertical farming will always be energy-intensive relative to solar-powered field agriculture.

Global Warming Potential

Life cycle assessments show vertical farming's global warming potential (GWP) ranges from 0.01 to 54 kg CO₂-equivalent per kg of produce, depending primarily on the electricity source. With renewable energy, GWP can be reduced 6-fold compared to fossil fuel electricity. The analysis suggests that decarbonizing the electricity grid is the single most impactful lever for improving the environmental performance of vertical farming.

Economic Viability Framework

A simple economic framework emerges from the calculations: vertical farming is viable when the product's market value ($/kg) exceeds the sum of energy costs + capital amortization + labor. For premium leafy greens at $5–15/kg retail, this threshold can be reached with electricity costs below $0.10–0.15/kWh. For commodity crops at $0.50–2/kg, vertical farming cannot currently compete economically.

Conclusions

Back-of-the-envelope calculations provide clarity on where vertical farming can and cannot deliver on its promises. The technology is genuinely transformative for high-value, fast-cycling crops — particularly leafy greens and herbs — in urban environments powered by renewable energy. For these applications, the combination of high land-use efficiency, water conservation, year-round production, and premium product quality creates a viable and increasingly competitive model.

However, vertical farming is not a universal solution to global food security. Staple crops that provide the bulk of human caloric intake are unlikely to be economically or energetically viable to produce vertically with current or near-future technology. The path forward for vertical farming lies in: continued reduction of LED energy costs (which have fallen 80% in the past decade), integration with renewable energy, focus on high-value specialty crops, and development of more energy-efficient cultivation practices.
